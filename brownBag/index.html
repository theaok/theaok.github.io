oct26 Michael D'Italia human agency: operationalization and measurement
Despite its presumed importance for the social sciences in general and the field of community development in particular, a recent meta-analysis by Cavazzoni et al. (2022) demonstrates a lack of consensus regarding how human agency is operationalized and measured. Building off their work, the present study attempts to develop a global tool for the assessment of personal human agency by a) situating the concept within the existing scholarship on human agency, b) identifying the characteristics of personal agency that are commonly agreed upon in the literature, and c) synthesizing a novel personal agency scale comprised of items that are identical or similar to those used in previous research. Using data from waves II and III of the Midlife in the United States (MIDUS) dataset, a preliminary list of personal agency items is developed and assessed using a combination of pairwise Spearman rank correlations, calculation of Cronbach’s alpha, the Keyser-Meyer-Olkin Measure of Sampling Adequacy (KMO) and Bartlett’s Test of Sphericity. After confirming the presence of meaningful relationships between items, exploratory factor analysis (EFA) is utilized to reduce the list to fifteen items that are salient on a general, dominant personal agency factor. The internal consistency and reliability of the resulting scale are confirmed before EFA is used again to test for underlying subfactors. This results in the identification of a two-subfactor model comprised of intrinsic and instrumental agency, the latter of which is tied to the concept of self-efficacy; an interpretable 3-factor model indicated that intrinsic agency can be further divided into the subfactors of autonomy and internal locus of control. Corresponding subscales are tested for internal reliability and consistency, then secondary analyses using alternative factor extraction and rotation methods and the calculation of the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) of scale items are used to test the robustness of findings. Finally, confirmatory factor analysis demonstrates the goodness of fit of the multifactor model. Combined, these analyses provide strong evidence that the proposed personal agency tool is an effective measure for assessing the concept.
